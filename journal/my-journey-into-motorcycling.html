My journey into motorcycling

Alex Fonseka 20 January 2025

“Ride like everybody is out to kill you”

— is a phrase every rider has heard sooner or later in their motorcycling journey. In the state of Victoria in Australia, motorcycles make up 4% of all registered vehicles, yet account for 17% of deaths and serious injuries. Similar figures can also be seen in the UK and a vast majority of cases are due to the negligence of other road users. The dangers associated with motorcycles are well-documented, widely acknowledged and backed up by statistics. With all that said, how did I possibly end up here?

To the surprise of friends and close associates, I bought a motorcycle early last year, incentivised by my declining mental health at the time. Many motorcyclists find that riding significantly contributes to stress reduction and improves their mental well-being. The demands of motorcycling necessitate constant alertness and the rider’s complete mental focus. It is, in its own unique way, a form of mindfulness meditation. After nearly a year of motorcycling, do I agree with these claims? To a great extent, I do. There’s also neuroscience research to back up these claims conducted by researchers at UCLA. They found that riding a motorcycle for 20 minutes resulted in decreased cortisol levels, greater tolerance to distractions, and enhanced sensory focus in participants.

On my motorcycling journey, I discovered that there are many other benefits to riding other than its therapeutic effects. A vast majority of these benefits are often understated and overshadowed by the prevailing negative connotations associated with motorcycling.

It’s not all doom and gloom

Riding a motorcycle creates a deep sense of unity between you and your surroundings. You feel the wind’s exhilarating rush, a sense of freedom. You feel the temperatures shift ever so slightly as you move from shade to sun. You’re fully enveloped in the sights, sounds and smells of your surroundings. All these elements come together to create the perfect adventure for travellers wanting to immerse themselves in their environment. If travelling solo isn’t your thing, you can always bring along a passenger to share the experience with. A motorcycle is an excellent choice for travel enthusiasts and adventure seekers.

A motorcycle can make commuting to work fun. When rush hour hits, roads transform into car parks and the crawl begins. On a motorcycle you won’t have to spend too long in asphalt purgatory. Here in Australia, motorcyclists can filter through stopped and slow moving traffic and it's completely legal if done safely. It’s also legal in the UK and most states of the USA. However, you may not exceed 30 km/h while doing so, as that is considered “lane-splitting” and is unlawful. Coming up with the most efficient strategy to get through traffic is a fun little exercise to play on your commute, as long as you do it safely and responsibly.

On a motorcycle, other road users will be more open to striking a conversation with you while stopped at the lights or in traffic. Me being an introvert, I usually do not initiate these conversations but there have been numerous instances where someone would put down their car window to compliment my bike, or a kid at the backseat would start waving. You will also get lots of nods from other motorcyclists and it’s always a great moment when that happens.

Riding a motorcycle also gives you unbeatable fuel efficiency and your commute contributes less emissions to the environment compared to the average car driver. A motorcycle may be a poor choice for hauling goods and people but for a white-collar office worker like myself, it makes for an excellent commuting machine.

Oh, and as an added bonus, riding a motorcycle burns a lot more calories than you might think. According to Dr. Michael, a radiologist who rides motorcycles, you can expect to burn up to 300 calories per every hour of riding. Although the actual number varies quite a bit based on the intensity of riding. For example, off-roaders and enduro riders will burn more calories than the average city commuter. Nevertheless, it’s a nice added perk for those wanting to stay fit.

Paying it forward

Being entirely self-taught, my motorcycling knowledge is largely derived from my own personal experience but also from the insights shared by fellow riders. Now I’m paying it forward by sharing my own experiences and some pro tips. I hope this may be beneficial to someone who’s new to riding or is interested in getting into it.

Like taming a horse

Learn and understand how your motorcycle behaves. Emphasis on “your” as every motorcycle is tuned differently and handles differently. The throttle response, the turning radius, the suspension rebound, and the biting point of the clutch are all different from model to model. Which is why it’s extremely important that you learn and understand the characteristics of your own motorcycle. The phrase “become one with the bike” gets thrown around a lot in motorcycling circles and for good reason. Even an experienced motorcyclist with many years of riding experience can be considered a beginner on a bike that they have never ridden before. Take your motorcycle to a quiet place like an empty car park where you can safely experiment with its controls and learn how it responds.

“Pick a bike that doesn’t freak you out” - Keanu Reeves

Obviously, before you do any of that, you need to pick a bike. You should take Keanu’s advice here (he’s always right, anyway).

Stick to a sub-400cc motorcycle if you’ve never ridden before. Anything higher than this will most likely overwhelm you. Here in Australia, there is also a CC limit imposed on motorcycles that are considered “learner approved” and as of writing, the most powerful learner approved motorcycle is the Yamaha MT-07 LAMS edition with an engine capacity of 655cc.

Aside from power, you should choose a bike with a seating position that you feel comfortable with. Sport bikes like the Kawasaki Ninja 400 typically require the rider to assume a forward-leaning position while naked bikes like the KTM 390 Duke or Kawasaki Z400 offer a more upright seating position. For everyday riding, I consider the upright seating position of a naked bike far more comfortable than the forward-leaning position of a sport bike. But your experience may differ. Have a play around with several different motorcycles at local dealerships and see which feels the most comfortable.

Don’t skimp out on gear

Get yourself a decent helmet from a reputable brand. I strongly advise a full face helmet with a Pinlock anti-fog insert. Beginner riders often neglect to buy protective equipment when they start out. Acknowledge that as a beginner, you are at your most vulnerable. So don’t skimp out on gear. Get yourself an abrasion resistant riding jacket, pants and boots. If the typical “rider look” isn’t your style, there are plenty of riding gear that masquerade as casual everyday clothes. But on the inside, they come with a lot of neat safety features like weather proofing, kevlar lining, knee guards and elbow guards. With options like these, you can express yourself while riding safely. Remember that the colour of the gear you wear matters. White or brightly coloured gear helps you stand out and be easily seen by other road users.

True skill is in riding slow, not fast

As it turns out, riding a motorcycle on a straight road at 100 km/h requires almost no effort. Anybody could do that. On a motorcycle equipped with cruise control, this could be done with your hands completely off the handlebars. Taking corners at 100 km/h is a different story though. As a motorcyclist, your mastery of slow speed manoeuvres is a strong indicator of your overall ability. Slow speed manoeuvres involve riding your motorcycle at walking speed (under 5 km/h) with both feet up and making tight U-turns, among other things. Pulling these off requires proper balance, good peripheral vision, and solid clutch control. Here in Australia, all riders must complete an advanced motorcycle training course and the accompanying practical test before they are allowed their full motorcycle license. And most advanced motorcycle courses spend an exorbitant amount of time teaching these concepts and making you practice them out on the range.

As a rider, I advise that you take matters into your own hands and spend some time practicing these manoeuvres at your local car park. It could be just a quick 20 minute session after you get back from work or uni. It will make you a safer rider as well as help you get through the advanced motorcycle test with ease.

Your choice of motorcycle matters when it comes to slow speed manoeuvres. If you choose to get a sport bike such as the Kawasaki Ninja 400, you will be at a disadvantage as the form factor and seating position make pulling off slow manoeuvres more difficult. Once again, I recommend picking a naked bike because they are much better suited for slow speed manoeuvres than sport bikes or cruisers. That’s in addition to their comfortable upright seating position which also makes them more suitable for everyday riding. The Kawasaki Z400, the Yamaha MT–03 and KTM’s 390 Duke are excellent beginner naked bikes. When taking the advanced motorcycle test in Australia, you can choose to bring your own motorcycle to do the test with. All three motorcycles I’ve listed previously are great choices for the test. Obviously, your ability as a rider matters greatly here, it’s not just about the type of motorcycle you ride.

Zero BAC, at all times

Aim for zero blood alcohol content if you need to ride your motorcycle. Riding a motorcycle requires far more finesse and skill than driving a car. Your sense of balance is critical while riding. Even a little bit of alcohol in your system can impair your senses and put you off balance. Don’t drink and ride, it’s not worth it.

Lane filter responsibly

Lane filtering with a motorcycle is both scary and rewarding at the same time. Only lane filter if you feel like it’s safe to do so and stay within a reasonable speed. In Australia, filtering at over 30 km/h is illegal and will get you pulled over. As a rule of thumb, I also avoid filtering next to large vehicles such as lorries and buses. If you are filtering up to the lights, I advise staying behind the first row of vehicles instead of filtering straight up to the front. The first row of vehicles will start moving as soon as the lights go green and if you happen to be in the middle of this row when that happens, it will put you in a dangerous situation. Note that drivers stuck in traffic are often distracted, so always pay close attention to other vehicles while filtering. Turning on your motorcycle’s hazard lights is a good way to draw attention to yourself and also signal to other road users that you are filtering. Make sure to turn them off afterwards. You will only get good at lane filtering the more you do it, but please do it responsibly and safely.

Minimise risks, maximise rewards

I’ll wrap things up by stating that while motorcycling has its risks, those risks can be mitigated by proper training, wearing safety gear, maintaining good awareness, and by following the rules. I would argue that a cyclist riding a push bicycle would be at a greater risk than a skilled motorcyclist who sticks to the above principles. Most push cyclists do not wear any protective gear, are completely exposed and do not have the capability to quickly get out of sticky situations, should one arise. Both cyclists and motorcyclists should take extra care at all times.

Motorcycles can be a great stress reliever, a highly efficient commuting machine and also perfect for travel enthusiasts wanting to immerse themselves in their surroundings. You just don’t get the same experience inside an air conditioned metal cage. I cannot understate the risks however. But I also think the benefits can outweigh the risks, if those risks are properly managed.

Thanks for making it to the end and I hope it was enlightening. I wish you all the best in your own motorcycling journey.
